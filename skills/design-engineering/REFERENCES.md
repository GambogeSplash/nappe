# References and lineage

Load this when work needs a point of view, not just competence. The native craft in `SKILL.md` and the discipline catalogs get you to correct. Reference is what gets you to *specific*: a design that could only belong to this product, reached by drawing on the deep history of how humans have made things across eras, places, and disciplines.

This file is the method for studying and borrowing well. The studies themselves live as dossiers in `references/`, each built to the template below.

---

## Why reference makes the work stronger

Left to a default, any maker converges on the average of everything seen most recently. For interfaces in 2026 that average is the SaaS look. The antidote is not more taste in the abstract; it is a specific, well-understood influence brought in on purpose. A borrowed constraint (a grid from Swiss design, a restraint from Dieter Rams, a planned dissonance from Memphis) is a force that pushes the work off the average toward something considered. Breadth of reference is the difference between work that is clean and work that has a position.

## The one rule: borrow the tension, not the texture

The failure mode of studying history is pastiche: 1981 becomes pastel squiggles, punk becomes ransom-note type, Swiss becomes Helvetica on a grid. That just trades the SaaS generic for an era costume. It is the same lazy move wearing a vintage jacket.

Study a movement to understand the **forces and tensions** that produced its look, then carry the *principle* onto your own surface. Translate, never transplant.

- Punk's lesson is not the ransom note. It is urgency through deliberate constraint and a refusal of polish. On a product that might mean raw system fonts, visible structure, and speed over decoration, with not one xerox texture in sight.
- Swiss design's lesson is not Helvetica. It is that a rigid grid plus restraint manufactures trust. That can live in a fintech dashboard that owns no mid-century typeface at all.
- Memphis's lesson is not the squiggle. It is that planned dissonance can feel alive where harmony feels dead. That might be one deliberately clashing accent in an otherwise disciplined palette.
- Japanese *ma* is not minimalism as a trend. It is negative space as an active material that gives the remaining elements their weight.

If a viewer can name your reference at a glance, you transplanted it. If the work simply feels considered and alive and they cannot say why, you translated it.

## How to read a source

Every dossier answers the same seven questions, in order. This is also the lens to run live on anything you study before it earns a dossier.

```
Context & forces   What produced this look, and what it reacted against:
                   technology of the day, economics, politics, materials,
                   the dominant style it was rebelling against.
Core principles    The transferable ideas, stated so they outlive the style.
Signature moves    Image-making, typography, color, composition, art direction.
Tensions           The productive constraints that defined it (a limit is
                   usually the real author of a style).
Key figures/works  Each with why it matters, not just a name to drop.
Transfer to UI     How each principle becomes a concrete interface decision,
                   written anti-pastiche.
Pitfalls           Exactly how this gets cheapened into cosplay, so we don't.
```

The most important box is **tensions**. Almost every era's signature look was forced by a constraint: punk by no budget and a photocopier, Swiss by postwar internationalism needing a neutral visual language, early-80s New Wave by the first cheap phototypesetting and then computers letting designers break rules that hot metal had enforced. Find the constraint and you find the why, and the why is what transfers.

## Translating across disciplines

The richest references are not other screens. Creativity in music, film, fashion, architecture, product, photography, and textile carries lessons that map cleanly onto interface dimensions. Pull from these on purpose.

| Source discipline | What it teaches | Maps to |
|---|---|---|
| Music (genre, tempo, dynamics) | Pacing, restraint versus maximalism, the build and the drop, silence as structure | Motion timing, information density, scroll rhythm, when to do nothing |
| Album art (Blue Note, Factory, 4AD) | Identity under tight constraint, image-making as a system | Hero imagery, the landing as a cover, a brand mark that carries weight |
| Film titles (Saul Bass, Kyle Cooper) | Sequencing, the reveal, type in motion, setting a mood in seconds | Onboarding, page transitions, narrative scroll, first-impression motion |
| Fashion | Silhouette, proportion, seasonal editing, texture and drape | Layout proportion, whitespace, the discipline of cutting, material feel |
| Architecture | Structure, materiality, circulation, light, scale | Grid, depth, navigation flow, hierarchy, the path through a product |
| Product design (Braun, Rams) | "Less but better," honest materials, affordance | Restraint, control clarity, removing the decorative, obvious affordances |
| Photography | Light, framing, tone, the decisive moment | Imagery direction, contrast, mood, what to crop in and out |
| Textiles (Ankara, Japanese, Islamic) | Pattern systems, repeat logic, rule-bound color | Background motifs, ornament discipline, palette construction, tiling |
| Editorial and magazines | The grid, pacing across spreads, the pull quote | Long-form layout, sectional rhythm, typographic hierarchy |

The translation is always principle to decision. Watch how a Saul Bass title withholds and then reveals, and build that withholding into a scroll sequence; do not put cut-paper shapes on the screen.

## Using a reference in real work

1. **Pick one primary lineage per project.** A second can season at most. Three influences average back into mush, which is the thing reference was supposed to defeat.
2. **Name it in the frame.** State the influence as part of the mental model, the way `SKILL.md` asks: "this reads like a Factory Records sleeve, restraint and system with one bold gesture," not "let's make it look retro."
3. **Translate every borrowed idea to a decision.** For each principle you take, write the specific interface choice it produces. If you cannot, you are reaching for texture, not tension.
4. **Verify it is not a costume.** Run the render-and-compare loop with one extra question: does this read as considered, or as cosplay? If a stranger would name the source instantly, pull it back.

## The atlas

A starting map of what is worth knowing, as pointers to study, not baked content. Each earns a full dossier in `references/` when a project calls for it. The aim is range across eras, places, and disciplines, deliberately wider than the Western tech canon.

**Movements and eras (Western lineage).** Bauhaus (form follows function, geometry, the first programmatic design education). Russian Constructivism (diagonal dynamism, red and black, photomontage as argument; Rodchenko, El Lissitzky). De Stijl (pure abstraction, primaries plus black and white; Mondrian). Swiss / International Typographic Style (the grid, objective typography, flush-left; Müller-Brockmann, Hofmann). Mid-century American (wit and concept; Paul Rand, Saul Bass, Lou Dorfsman) and Push Pin Studios' illustrative eclecticism (Milton Glaser). Psychedelia (fluid type, vibrating complementaries). Punk and post-punk DIY (the photocopier aesthetic; Jamie Reid). New Wave and Swiss-breaking (Wolfgang Weingart's deconstruction, April Greiman's hybrid and early-digital imagery, Dan Friedman). Postmodern and Memphis (planned dissonance; Ettore Sottsass, 1981) with its graphic heirs Neville Brody (The Face), Peter Saville (Factory), Vaughan Oliver (4AD). 90s deconstruction and grunge (David Carson, Ray Gun) and rave graphics (The Designers Republic). Y2K techno-optimism (chrome, gradients, blobs).

**Figures worth a deep read.** Josef Müller-Brockmann, Paul Rand, Saul Bass, Massimo Vignelli, Milton Glaser, Wolfgang Weingart, April Greiman, Neville Brody, Peter Saville, Paula Scher, David Carson, Ettore Sottsass.

**Cross-discipline touchstones.** Blue Note covers (Reid Miles), Hipgnosis and Factory and 4AD record sleeves, Saul Bass and Kyle Cooper title sequences, Braun under Dieter Rams, the great fashion houses by era, documentary and fashion photography.

**Non-Western traditions (most design writing skips these; that is the edge).**
- **Japanese.** *Ma* (negative space as active material), *notan* (light and dark balance), *mon* (family crests as the original minimal marks), ukiyo-e composition; Tadanori Yokoo, Ikko Tanaka (Swiss rigor fused with Japanese form), Kenya Hara (emptiness as a strategy, MUJI).
- **West African and Nigerian.** Adinkra (Akan symbol system, each glyph a proverb), Nsibidi (the ancient ideographic script of southeastern Nigeria), Yoruba aesthetics and Adire and Aso-oke textiles, Ankara and Dutch-wax pattern and color logic, Uli line motifs and the Nsukka school (Uche Okeke). Contemporary: Laolu Senbanjo's Afromysterics, Osborne Macharia's Afrofuturist art direction.
- **Islamic.** Geometric tessellation, the arabesque, calligraphy as image, infinite pattern, and the abstraction that aniconism forced into high art.
- **Latin American and Eastern European.** Mexican muralism, Cuban revolutionary posters (OSPAAAL), the Polish Poster School (painterly, metaphorical, hand-drawn), Brazilian modernism.

## Building a dossier

To study any entry deeply, run the **`deep-research`** harness so the work is sourced, cross-checked, and cited rather than recalled from a vague memory of the look. Pass it the seven-box template above as the required output shape, ask it to extract transferable principles and concrete interface transfers (never swatches), and save the result to `references/<slug>.md`. Each dossier should end with the same warning its subject deserves: the precise way it gets cheapened, so we borrow the tension and leave the costume on the rack.
